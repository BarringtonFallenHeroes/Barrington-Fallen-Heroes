Private Jack Allin

Private Jack Allin was a formerly enslaved resident of Barrington, Rhode Island, who gained his freedom through military service in the Continental Army during the American Revolution. He served in the renowned 1st Rhode Island Regiment and died while serving his country at Valley Forge on May 18, 1778.

Early Life in Barrington

Jack Allin and his brother Richard Allin were enslaved members of the prominent Allin household of Barrington. Both appear in the estate inventory of Matthew Allin in 1761, where they were listed among the enslaved people who formed part of the estate's property holdings.

Following the division of the estate, Jack became associated with Captain Thomas Allin's household while Richard remained with Matthew Allin's branch of the family. The brothers spent their youth in Barrington during a period when slavery was still legal in Rhode Island.

Freedom Through Military Service

On May 19, 1777, Jack Allin was freed from slavery in order to enlist in the Continental Army. He joined Captain Thomas Cole's Company of the 1st Rhode Island Regiment, one of the most significant military units of the American Revolution.

The regiment included Black, Native American, and white soldiers serving together in the cause of independence. Rhode Island's policy of permitting enslaved men to enlist in exchange for freedom offered men like Jack the opportunity to secure both personal liberty and American independence through military service.

Military Service

Jack enlisted alongside seven other Barrington men to help fill the town's military quota. Among those serving with him were his brother Richard Allin, fellow formerly enslaved soldiers Thomas Reynolds and Pomp Watson, free Indigenous soldier Joseph Sochorose, and several white soldiers from Barrington.

During 1777, the regiment campaigned in New York and New Jersey and participated in the successful defense of Fort Mercer (Red Bank) against Hessian forces on October 22, 1777. Later that year, the regiment joined General George Washington's army at Valley Forge, Pennsylvania.

Valley Forge

The winter encampment at Valley Forge became one of the defining experiences of the Revolutionary War. Soldiers endured severe shortages of food, clothing, shelter, and medical supplies while disease spread throughout the camp.

Military records show that Jack Allin was listed as sick but still present with his company during April 1778. By May he had been transferred to a detachment composed largely of sick soldiers.

On May 18, 1778, after nearly one year of military service, Private Jack Allin died at Valley Forge. He was one of approximately 1,700 Continental soldiers who lost their lives to disease, malnutrition, or exposure during the encampment. Like many of those who perished there, he was likely buried in an unmarked grave near the cantonment.

Legacy

Jack Allin never returned to Barrington. His sacrifice represents the service of formerly enslaved African Americans who fought for a nation founded on the ideals of liberty while seeking freedom for themselves.

His story is an important part of Barrington's Revolutionary War heritage and serves as a reminder that the struggle for American independence was carried by individuals from many backgrounds. Through his service and ultimate sacrifice, Jack Allin helped secure the freedoms that future generations would enjoy.

Today, Private Jack Allin is honored among Barrington's Fallen Heroes for giving his life in service to the United States during the American Revolution.
