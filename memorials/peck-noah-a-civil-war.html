Remembering Noah A. Peck

Noah A. Peck was among the earliest Rhode Islanders to volunteer for service following the outbreak of the Civil War. He would serve nearly two years before suffering mortal wounds during one of the war's most famous battles.

Military Service

Peck enrolled for service on June 5, 1861, and was mustered into Company D, 2nd Rhode Island Volunteer Infantry later that month.

The 2nd Rhode Island Infantry served with the Army of the Potomac and participated in many of the Eastern Theater's most important campaigns.

Demonstrating leadership and dedication to duty, Peck was promoted to Corporal on April 1, 1862, and later attained the rank of Sergeant.

Battle of Chancellorsville

In the spring of 1863, the Army of the Potomac advanced against Confederate forces near Chancellorsville, Virginia.

The Battle of Chancellorsville, fought from April 30 through May 6, 1863, is remembered as General Robert E. Lee's most audacious victory, despite being heavily outnumbered by Union forces.

On May 3, 1863, Sgt. Peck was severely wounded during the battle while serving with the 2nd Rhode Island Infantry.

Final Sacrifice

Following his wounding, Peck was evacuated to a United States military hospital where surgeons were forced to amputate his left leg in an attempt to save his life.

Despite receiving medical treatment, his condition continued to deteriorate.

On June 2, 1863, Sgt. Noah A. Peck died at Campbell Military Hospital from complications resulting from his wounds and amputation.

He was 34 years old.

Burial and Memorial

Sgt. Peck was returned home and laid to rest in Forest Chapel Cemetery, where his grave continues to honor his sacrifice and service to the Union.

Legacy

Noah A. Peck is remembered among Barrington's Civil War fallen and among the soldiers of the Army of the Potomac who endured some of the war's fiercest fighting.

Though he survived the battlefield itself, he ultimately gave his life as a result of wounds sustained while helping preserve the Union during one of the Civil War's defining campaigns.
