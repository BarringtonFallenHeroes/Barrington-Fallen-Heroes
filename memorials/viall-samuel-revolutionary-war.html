Remembering Samuel Viall

Samuel Viall was one of Barrington's earliest Revolutionary War patriots. A veteran of both artillery and naval service, he gave his life while defending Rhode Island during the struggle for American independence.

Military Service

Samuel Viall served as a soldier in Captain Samuel Bosworth's Artillery Company and answered the alarm at Bristol on April 1, 1776, during a period of heightened British naval activity in Narragansett Bay.

The Viall family was among the longstanding families of Barrington and the East Bay region. Samuel Viall's service reflected the commitment of many local citizens who took up arms to defend their communities during the Revolution.

Rhode Island's coastal communities were particularly vulnerable to British raids, making local artillery and maritime defenses critical to the Patriot cause.

Naval Service

In 1777, Viall served as First Lieutenant aboard the row-galley Spitfire, one of several armed vessels employed by Rhode Island to defend its waterways against British naval operations.

Row-galleys were small, maneuverable fighting craft capable of operating in the shallow waters of Narragansett Bay and its tributaries. They played an important role in protecting local commerce and challenging British control of the region.

Defending Rhode Island Waters

In March 1777, Joseph Carlo Mauran of Barrington was selected as master of the row-galley Spitfire, which was assigned to operate in Rhode Island waters. Samuel Viall, identified by Bicknell as being of Wannamoisett, served as the vessel's First Lieutenant.

The Spitfire formed part of Rhode Island's effort to defend Narragansett Bay and the East Bay communities from British naval activity. Operating in shallow coastal waters, row-galleys provided a mobile defense force capable of carrying artillery, transporting personnel, and responding rapidly to enemy movements.

During this period, Barrington and neighboring communities were actively preparing for potential British attacks. The General Assembly stationed troops for local defense, distributed gunpowder to vulnerable towns, and strengthened military readiness throughout the region.

Circumstances of Death

While serving aboard the row-galley Spitfire in the Kickemuit River, Viall was killed in an accidental explosion of gunpowder.

The accident occurred on April 22, 1777, during active military operations associated with Rhode Island's defense against British naval forces. Though not killed in combat, Viall died while performing military duty in service to the Patriot cause.

He was in the forty-eighth year of his age.

His death illustrates the many dangers faced by Revolutionary War servicemen. In addition to enemy action, soldiers and sailors routinely worked with black powder, artillery, and munitions under hazardous conditions where accidents could prove fatal.

Burial and Memorial

Samuel Viall was buried in Allin Yard Cemetery in West Barrington.

His gravestone bears the following inscription:

"Lost his life by explosion of powder on board of a galley
in the Revolutionary War: April 22nd, 1777, in the 48th
year of his age."

The inscription preserves the memory of a patriot who died while serving Rhode Island during the earliest years of the nation's struggle for independence.

Legacy

Samuel Viall is remembered among Barrington's Revolutionary War fallen and among the Rhode Islanders who defended their communities by both land and sea.

His service illustrates the importance of local maritime forces in protecting Narragansett Bay and resisting British operations throughout the Revolutionary War.

As one of Barrington's earliest Revolutionary War casualties, Samuel Viall's sacrifice demonstrates that the struggle for independence touched even the small communities of Rhode Island.

Today, Samuel Viall rests in Allin Yard Cemetery, where his gravestone continues to commemorate a Barrington patriot who gave his life while defending Rhode Island during the earliest years of the American Revolution.

Historical Note

The Kickemuit River, where Viall lost his life, was an important waterway connecting Barrington, Warren, and Bristol. During the Revolutionary War it served as both a refuge and operating area for Patriot vessels engaged in defending the East Bay communities.
