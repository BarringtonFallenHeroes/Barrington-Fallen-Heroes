Remembering Samuel Viall

Samuel Viall was one of Barrington's earliest Revolutionary War patriots. A veteran of both artillery and naval service, he gave his life while defending Rhode Island during the struggle for American independence.

Military Service

Samuel Viall served as a soldier in Captain Samuel Bosworth's Artillery Company and answered the alarm at Bristol on April 1, 1776, during a period of heightened British naval activity in Narragansett Bay.

Rhode Island's coastal communities were particularly vulnerable to British raids, making local artillery and maritime defenses critical to the Patriot cause.

Naval Service

In 1777, Viall served as First Lieutenant aboard the row-galley Spitfire, one of several armed vessels employed by Rhode Island to defend its waterways against British naval operations.

Row-galleys were small, maneuverable fighting craft capable of operating in the shallow waters of Narragansett Bay and its tributaries. They played an important role in protecting local commerce and challenging British control of the region.

Circumstances of Death

While serving aboard the Spitfire in the Kickemuit River, Viall was killed in an accidental explosion of gunpowder.

The accident occurred on April 2, 1777, while the vessel was engaged in military service during the Revolutionary War.

He was in the forty-eighth year of his age.

Burial and Memorial

Samuel Viall was buried in Allin Yard Cemetery in West Barrington.

His gravestone bears the following inscription:

"Lost his life by explosion of powder on board of a galley in the Revolutionary War: April 22nd, 1777, in the 48th year of his age."

The inscription preserves the memory of a patriot who died while serving Rhode Island during the earliest years of the nation's struggle for independence.

Legacy

Samuel Viall is remembered among Barrington's Revolutionary War fallen and among the Rhode Islanders who defended their communities by both land and sea.

His service illustrates the importance of local maritime forces in protecting Narragansett Bay and resisting British operations throughout the Revolutionary War.

Historical Note

The Kickemuit River, where Viall lost his life, was an important waterway connecting Barrington, Warren, and Bristol. During the Revolutionary War it served as both a refuge and operating area for Patriot vessels engaged in defending the East Bay communities.
