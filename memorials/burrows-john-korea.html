Remembering John K. Burrows

First Lieutenant John K. Burrows was a World War II veteran, Barrington resident, husband, father, and United States Army officer who gave his life as a prisoner of war during the Korean War.

Captured near Unsan, North Korea, during the opening phase of the Chinese Communist Forces intervention, he died in captivity at Pyoktong Prison Camp on January 31, 1951.

Early Life

John K. Burrows was born on December 15, 1921, in North Providence, Rhode Island. He was the son of an English immigrant father and a mother from California.

He was raised in Johnston, Rhode Island, before entering military service during the Second World War.

World War II Service

Burrows enlisted in the Rhode Island Army National Guard in 1941 and served during World War II in the Pacific with the 103rd Field Artillery Regiment.

Like many men of his generation, he returned from World War II having already served his country in a global conflict before being called again during the Korean War.

Barrington Connection

After World War II, Burrows married Esther Tiffany of Barrington. The couple had a son, Stephen, in 1946 and made their home at 17 Houghton Street in Barrington.

Through his wife, family, and residence, Burrows became part of Barrington's postwar community before returning to military service during the Korean War.

Korean War Service

During the Korean War, First Lieutenant Burrows served as a tank commander with Company L, 3rd Battalion, 8th Cavalry Regiment, 1st Cavalry Division.

The 8th Cavalry Regiment was heavily engaged during the United Nations advance into North Korea in the fall of 1950. At the time, many believed the war might soon end, but Chinese Communist Forces had begun crossing the Yalu River and entering the conflict in large numbers.

The Battle Near Unsan

On November 2, 1950, Burrows was captured while fighting near Unsan, North Korea, approximately fifty miles south of the Chinese border.

His battalion was cut off during the massive Chinese counteroffensive. Rather than withdraw immediately, elements of the regiment continued to fight under intense pressure. The 8th Cavalry Regiment suffered devastating losses, with hundreds of men killed, wounded, missing, or captured.

Burrows became one of the American soldiers taken prisoner during this sudden and costly phase of the Korean War.

Prisoner of War

After his capture, Burrows was taken to Pyoktong Prison Camp in North Korea.

Conditions for American prisoners were severe. Captives endured inadequate food, limited medical care, exposure to harsh winter weather, disease, and chronic malnutrition.

Burrows suffered from beriberi, a disease caused by severe nutritional deficiency, while imprisoned.

Circumstances of Death

First Lieutenant John K. Burrows died as a prisoner of war at Pyoktong Prison Camp on January 31, 1951.

Official records identify beriberi and malnutrition as the cause of death.

He was twenty-nine years old.

His remains were never recovered. He is believed to have been buried near the Yalu River with other American prisoners of war who died in captivity.

Burial and Memorial

Because his remains were not recovered, First Lieutenant Burrows is memorialized on the Courts of the Missing at the Honolulu Memorial in the National Memorial Cemetery of the Pacific in Hawaii.

Barrington honors his memory among the community's fallen heroes who gave their lives in service to the United States.

Awards and Honors

For his service, leadership, and sacrifice, First Lieutenant Burrows received multiple military honors.

  • Bronze Star Medal
  • Prisoner of War Medal
  • Combat Infantryman Badge
  • Korean Service Medal
  • United Nations Service Medal
  • National Defense Service Medal
  • Korean Presidential Unit Citation
  • Republic of Korea War Service Medal

Legacy

John K. Burrows survived World War II and returned home to begin a new life with his wife Esther and their young son, Stephen, in Barrington.

Called back to military service during the Korean War, he once again answered his nation's call. His death in captivity left behind a young bride and a son who would grow up without knowing his father.

Stephen Burrows passed away in 2009, having lived his life without the father who had been lost in Korea.

Today, First Lieutenant John K. Burrows is remembered among Barrington's Fallen Heroes and among the Americans who endured captivity and gave their lives during the Korean War.
