Remembering Albert W. Adams

Albert W. Adams was the son of Earl R. Adams and Kathleen Healy Adams of 132 Bay Spring Avenue in Barrington, Rhode Island. He attended Peck High School and was a member of St. Luke's Church before entering military service during World War II.

Barrington Connection

Albert grew up in the Bay Spring section of Barrington and answered his nation's call during World War II by enlisting in the United States Navy.

The Adams family paid a profound price during World War II. Albert's younger brother, Earl R. Adams, Jr., lost his life aboard the Liberty Ship SS Henry Bacon at only sixteen years of age. The family ultimately sacrificed two sons in service to their country.

Military Service

Albert served as a Baker 3rd Class aboard the submarine USS Trout (SS-202), one of the most successful submarines of the Pacific War. During her distinguished wartime career, USS Trout completed numerous combat patrols and participated in operations throughout the Pacific Theater.

Final Patrol of USS Trout

On February 8, 1944, USS Trout departed on her eleventh and final war patrol. After refueling at Midway Island, the submarine proceeded toward the East China Sea to attack Japanese shipping.

Japanese records examined after the war indicate that on February 29, 1944, a submarine operating in Trout's assigned patrol area attacked Convoy Matsu No. 1. During the attack, the transport Sakito Maru, carrying elements of the Japanese 18th Infantry Regiment, was sunk and another vessel was heavily damaged.

Following the attack, Japanese destroyers detected the submarine and launched a series of depth-charge attacks. Oil and debris were observed on the surface, leading historians to conclude that USS Trout was lost during the engagement.

Having failed to return from patrol, USS Trout was declared missing in action on April 7, 1944. On April 8, 1945, the submarine was officially declared lost with all eighty-one officers and crewmen aboard.

Circumstances of Death

Albert W. Adams was among the eighty-one sailors lost when USS Trout failed to return from her final patrol. He was twenty-four years old.

Like his shipmates, Albert remains on Eternal Patrol, remembered by the submarine service, his hometown of Barrington, and the nation he served.

Family Sacrifice

Few Barrington families endured a greater wartime loss than the Adams family. During World War II they lost both sons, Earl R. Adams, Jr. and Albert W. Adams, in separate incidents while serving their nation.

Albert was lost aboard USS Trout in the Pacific, while Earl perished aboard SS Henry Bacon off the coast of Norway. Their story remains one of the most poignant examples of sacrifice preserved in Barrington's military history.

Burial and Memorial

Because USS Trout was lost at sea with all hands, Albert W. Adams has no known grave. He is memorialized on the Tablets of the Missing and is honored by his family, community, and nation.

Today he is remembered as one of Barrington's World War II fallen and as one of eighty-one sailors who gave their lives aboard USS Trout during her final patrol in the Pacific.
