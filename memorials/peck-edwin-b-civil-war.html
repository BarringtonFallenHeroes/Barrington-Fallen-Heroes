Remembering Edwin B. Peck

Edwin B. Peck was among the earliest Barrington residents to answer the Union's call to arms following the outbreak of the Civil War.

Military Service

Peck enrolled for service in Barrington, Rhode Island, on October 21, 1861.

He was mustered into Company E, 5th Rhode Island Heavy Artillery on December 16, 1861.

The regiment was assigned to General Ambrose Burnside's North Carolina Expedition, one of the Union's earliest amphibious operations of the Civil War.

Burnside's North Carolina Expedition

In early 1862, Union forces embarked on a campaign to seize strategic Confederate positions along the North Carolina coast, including Hatteras Inlet and Roanoke Island.

The expedition represented an important effort to establish Union control of coastal waterways and support the broader naval blockade of the Confederacy.

Circumstances of Death

While participating in Burnside's Expedition near Hatteras Inlet, North Carolina, Pvt. Peck became ill.

On February 9, 1862, he died of disease while serving with his regiment during the expedition.

Like thousands of Civil War soldiers, Peck fell victim to disease, which claimed more lives during the conflict than combat itself.

Legacy

Edwin B. Peck is remembered among Barrington's Civil War fallen and among the Rhode Islanders who participated in the Union's early campaigns along the Atlantic Coast.

Though he never lived to see the outcome of the war, his service formed part of the larger effort that ultimately preserved the Union.
