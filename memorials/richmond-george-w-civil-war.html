Remembering George W. Richmond

George W. Richmond was among the young men of Barrington who answered the call to preserve the Union during the American Civil War. Though only eighteen years old, he volunteered for military service and ultimately gave his life as a result of wounds received in battle.

Military Service

Richmond enrolled for service on September 24, 1862, and was mustered into Company E, 12th Rhode Island Volunteer Infantry on October 13, 1862.

The 12th Rhode Island Infantry was a nine-month regiment organized to reinforce Union armies during a critical period of the war.

Battle of Fredericksburg

On December 13, 1862, Pvt. Richmond fought in the Battle of Fredericksburg, Virginia.

Fredericksburg became one of the bloodiest and most costly Union defeats of the Civil War. Union forces repeatedly assaulted heavily fortified Confederate positions on Marye's Heights and suffered devastating casualties.

During the fighting, Richmond was severely wounded while serving with the 12th Rhode Island Infantry.

Final Sacrifice

Following the battle, Richmond was evacuated to Carver Hospital in Washington, D.C., where he received treatment for his wounds.

Despite the efforts of military surgeons, he never fully recovered.

On February 15, 1863, Pvt. George W. Richmond died from wounds received at Fredericksburg.

He was only eighteen years old.

Burial and Memorial

Pvt. Richmond was returned home and buried in Forest Chapel Cemetery in Barrington.

His gravestone bears the following inscription:

"Actuated by a sense of duty, he promptly volunteered in defense of our Union and offered up his life for his country."

Legacy

George W. Richmond is remembered among Barrington's Civil War fallen and among the young volunteers whose courage carried them into some of the war's most brutal fighting.

Though his military service lasted only a few months, his sacrifice reflected a profound commitment to duty and to the preservation of the United States.
