Remembering Winchester Bicknell

Winchester Bicknell served both in the Rhode Island militia and later as a seaman aboard an American privateer during the Revolutionary War. His story reflects the dangers faced not only by soldiers on land but also by those who carried the fight to the seas in support of American independence.

Early Life

Winchester Bicknell was born on March 31, 1761, the son of Joshua and Jerusha Bicknell of Barrington, Rhode Island.

He grew up during a period of increasing conflict between Great Britain and its American colonies and reached military age as the Revolution unfolded.

Military Service

Bicknell first served in Captain Viall Allen's militia company and later served as a private in Captain Philip Traffern's Company of Colonel Topham's Regiment during the campaigns of 1778 and 1779.

Rhode Island's militia played an important role in defending local communities and supporting Continental Army operations throughout the war.

Privateer Service

In May 1782, Bicknell entered maritime service as a seaman aboard the American privateer Chance, which sailed from Providence.

The vessel was owned by the prominent Providence merchants Clark and Nightingale and commanded by Captain Daniel Aborn of Pawtucket. Armed with twelve cannon and carrying a crew of approximately sixty-five men, the Chance sought to disrupt British shipping and commerce during the final stages of the war.

Capture by the British

Only days after departing Providence, the Chance was intercepted and captured by HMS Belisarius, a British warship commanded by Captain Graves and armed with twenty-six guns.

Bicknell and his fellow crewmen became prisoners of war and were transported to New York.

The Jersey Prison Ship

Following their capture, the prisoners were confined aboard the notorious British prison ship Jersey, anchored in Wallabout Bay near present-day Brooklyn, New York.

Originally a seventy-four-gun ship of the line, the Jersey had been stripped of its armament and converted into a floating prison.

Conditions aboard the vessel were notoriously brutal. Overcrowding, disease, inadequate food, and poor sanitation claimed the lives of thousands of American prisoners during the Revolutionary War.

Contemporary accounts described the prison ship as one of the most feared places in British custody and a symbol of the suffering endured by American prisoners of war.

Circumstances of Death

Winchester Bicknell died while held as a prisoner aboard the prison ship Jersey.

He was twenty-one years old.

His death came only months before the conclusion of the Revolutionary War and the ultimate recognition of American independence.

Burial and Memorial

Winchester Bicknell is buried in Prince's Hill Cemetery in Barrington, Rhode Island.

Legacy

Winchester Bicknell is remembered among Barrington's Revolutionary War fallen and among the many Americans who suffered and died in British captivity during the struggle for independence.

His story serves as a reminder that the cost of the Revolution extended far beyond the battlefield and included the hardships endured by prisoners of war held under harsh conditions.

Historical Note

The experiences of prisoners aboard the Jersey prison ship were later documented in memoirs and historical accounts, including the recollections of Captain Thomas Dring. These accounts provide valuable insight into one of the most difficult chapters of the Revolutionary War.
