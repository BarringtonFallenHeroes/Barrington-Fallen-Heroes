Remembering Winchester Bicknell

Winchester Bicknell served both in the Rhode Island militia and later as a seaman aboard an American privateer during the Revolutionary War. Captured by the British and confined aboard the prison ship Jersey, he survived captivity but died shortly after his release, at only twenty-one years old.

Early Life

Winchester Bicknell was born on March 31, 1761, the son of Joshua and Jerusha Bicknell of Barrington, Rhode Island.

The Bicknell family was among Barrington's early families and contributed to the civic, religious, and military life of the town throughout the colonial and Revolutionary periods.

Winchester grew up during a period of increasing conflict between Great Britain and its American colonies and reached military age as the Revolution unfolded.

Military Service

Bicknell first served in Captain Viall Allen's militia company and later served as a private in Captain Philip Traffern's Company of Colonel Topham's Regiment during the campaigns of 1778 and 1779.

Rhode Island's militia played an important role in defending local communities and supporting Continental Army operations throughout the war.

Privateer Service

In May 1782, Bicknell entered maritime service as a seaman aboard the American privateer Chance, which sailed from Providence.

Privateers were privately owned vessels authorized by the Patriot government to attack enemy shipping. These vessels helped disrupt British commerce and supplemented the limited naval resources available to the American cause.

The Chance was owned by the prominent Providence merchants Clark and Nightingale and commanded by Captain Daniel Aborn of Pawtucket. Armed with twelve cannon and carrying a crew of approximately sixty-five men, the Chance sailed during the final stages of the war.

Capture by the British

Only days after departing Providence, the Chance was intercepted and captured by HMS Belisarius, a British warship commanded by Captain Graves and armed with twenty-six guns.

Bicknell and his fellow crewmen became prisoners of war and were transported to New York.

The Jersey Prison Ship

Following their capture, the prisoners were confined aboard the notorious British prison ship Jersey, anchored in Wallabout Bay near present-day Brooklyn, New York.

Originally a seventy-four-gun ship of the line, the Jersey had been stripped of its armament and converted into a floating prison.

Conditions aboard the vessel were notoriously brutal. Overcrowding, disease, inadequate food, contaminated water, and poor sanitation claimed the lives of thousands of American prisoners during the Revolutionary War.

Contemporary accounts described the prison ship as one of the most feared places in British custody and a symbol of the suffering endured by American prisoners of war.

Circumstances of Death

Although Winchester Bicknell survived confinement aboard the prison ship Jersey and was released from British captivity, his ordeal was not over.

According to Thomas Williams Bicknell's A History of Barrington, Rhode Island, Winchester Bicknell died on a boat in the Barrington River after his release from the deadly prison ship.

He was twenty-one years old.

While the exact medical cause of death is unknown, his death appears closely connected to the hardships he endured as a prisoner of war. Like many former prisoners, he likely returned home weakened by disease, exposure, malnutrition, or the harsh conditions of captivity.

Burial and Memorial

Winchester Bicknell is buried in Prince's Hill Cemetery in Barrington, Rhode Island.

His gravestone bears a moving epitaph preserved by Thomas Williams Bicknell:

"Alas, dear friend, no sooner came
Thy earthly usefulness to bloom,
But death has cropt thy tender bud,
And laid thee in this mournful tomb."

Legacy

Winchester Bicknell is remembered among Barrington's Revolutionary War fallen and among the many Americans who suffered in British captivity during the struggle for independence.

His story shows that the cost of the Revolution extended beyond the battlefield. For some patriots, imprisonment, disease, and suffering continued even after release from enemy hands.

Today, Winchester Bicknell rests in Prince's Hill Cemetery, where his memorial continues to testify to the sacrifice of a young Barrington patriot whose life was cut short in the service of American independence.

Historical Note

The experiences of prisoners aboard the Jersey prison ship were later documented in memoirs and historical accounts, including the recollections of Captain Thomas Dring. These accounts provide valuable insight into one of the most difficult chapters of the Revolutionary War.
