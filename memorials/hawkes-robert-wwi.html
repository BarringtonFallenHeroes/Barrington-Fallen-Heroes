Remembering Robert Hawkes

Robert Hawkes was born on October 22, 1890, in County Tyrone, Northern Ireland. Like many young immigrants seeking greater opportunity, he came to the United States in 1909 and eventually settled in Barrington, Rhode Island.

Barrington Connection

Hawkes found employment as a gardener working for Frederick Ballou at his residence on Nayatt Road in Barrington.

While employed by Ballou, he resided with his brother-in-law, George Hamilton, on Rumstick Road.

Military Service

As a British subject, Hawkes answered the call to military service through the Canadian Expeditionary Force.

He enlisted in the 116th Infantry Battalion and was later assigned to the 9th Infantry Brigade, 3rd Canadian Division, serving on the Western Front in France.

The Second Battle of Arras

On August 27, 1918, during the opening day of the Second Battle of Arras, Pvt. Hawkes was killed in action.

The battle formed part of the Allied Hundred Days Offensive, a series of attacks that ultimately forced Germany to seek an armistice and brought the First World War to an end.

Family Sacrifice

Hawkes left behind his new bride, Mary Hawkes of New Grafton, Nova Scotia.

He was also survived by his sister, Elizabeth Hawkes of Westerly, Rhode Island, and other family members who remained in County Tyrone, Northern Ireland.

Burial and Memorial

Pvt. Robert Hawkes is buried in the Canadian War Cemetery at Vimy, France.

He rests among thousands of Canadian soldiers who gave their lives during the campaign that helped secure Allied victory in the final months of the war.

Legacy

Robert Hawkes is remembered among Barrington's World War I fallen and among the many immigrants who made new lives in America yet ultimately answered the call to serve the nations of their birth.
