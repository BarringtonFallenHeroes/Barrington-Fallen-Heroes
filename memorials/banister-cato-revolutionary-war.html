Private Cato Banister

Private Cato Banister served in the Rhode Island Regiment during the American Revolution. He is remembered as one of the African American patriots who fought for American independence in one of the most distinguished units of the Continental Army.

Rhode Island was unique among the colonies in permitting enslaved men to enlist for military service, with freedom granted in exchange for service to the Patriot cause. Banister's service reflects both the struggle for American independence and the personal pursuit of liberty by African Americans who answered the nation's call.

Military Service

Banister served as a Private in the Rhode Island Regiment, a unit that included Black, Native American, and white soldiers serving together in defense of the American cause. Muster records place him in the regiment under the command of Colonel Christopher Greene.

Death in Service

On May 14, 1781, Banister was killed during the Battle of Pines Bridge in New York. The engagement occurred when Loyalist forces launched a surprise attack against American troops guarding the strategic crossing over the Croton River.

Colonel Christopher Greene and several members of the Rhode Island Regiment were killed during the fighting. Rhode Island Historical Society records list Private Cato Banister as "killed" on May 14, 1781.

Legacy

Private Cato Banister's sacrifice represents the courage and commitment of the African American soldiers who fought and died for the cause of American independence. His service reminds us that the struggle for liberty was carried forward by men from many backgrounds who shared a common dedication to freedom.

Today, Cato Banister is honored among Barrington's Fallen Heroes for giving his life in military service during the Revolutionary War.
