Remembering John B. Mathewson

John B. Mathewson was among the first Barrington men to answer the call to preserve the Union during the American Civil War. Though only eighteen years old, he volunteered for military service during the opening months of the conflict.

Military Service

Mathewson enrolled and was mustered into Battery E, 1st Rhode Island Light Artillery on September 30, 1861.

The 1st Rhode Island Light Artillery was among the state's most distinguished military organizations and would serve throughout many of the major campaigns fought by the Army of the Potomac.

Early Campaign Service

During the autumn of 1861, Mathewson participated in operations near Bolivar Heights, Virginia, seeing action there on October 16, 1861.

Bolivar Heights formed part of the defensive positions surrounding Harpers Ferry and was strategically important in controlling movement through the Shenandoah Valley.

Circumstances of Death

Like thousands of Civil War soldiers, Mathewson faced an enemy more deadly than Confederate bullets. Disease swept through military camps during the war and claimed more lives than combat.

On January 22, 1862, Corporal Mathewson died of disease at the camp hospital near Fort Lyon, Virginia.

He was only eighteen years old.

Burial and Memorial

Corporal Mathewson is buried in Forest Chapel Cemetery, where his grave remains a lasting reminder of Barrington's sacrifice during the Civil War.

Inscribed upon his tombstone are the Latin words:

"Dulce et decorum est pro patria mori."

Translated, the phrase means:

"It is sweet and fitting to die for one's country."

Legacy

John B. Mathewson is remembered among Barrington's Civil War fallen and among the young volunteers who stepped forward at the beginning of the conflict to defend the Union.

Though his service was brief, his sacrifice became part of the larger struggle that ultimately preserved the United States and ended slavery.
