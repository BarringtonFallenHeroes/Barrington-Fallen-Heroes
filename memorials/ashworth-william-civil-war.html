Remembering William Ashworth

William Ashworth answered the call to preserve the Union during the American Civil War, serving as a private in Company K, 7th Rhode Island Volunteer Infantry.

Military Service

Ashworth enrolled for service on August 5, 1862, and was mustered into Company K, 7th Rhode Island Volunteer Infantry on September 6, 1862.

The 7th Rhode Island served in several important campaigns in the Western Theater and earned a reputation for steadfast service under difficult conditions.

Vicksburg Campaign

During the summer of 1863, Pvt. Ashworth participated in the operations against Vicksburg, Mississippi, one of the most decisive campaigns of the Civil War.

Following the fall of Vicksburg on July 4, 1863, he also took part in operations against Jackson, Mississippi, helping secure Union control of the Mississippi River and divide the Confederacy.

He participated in the Siege of Vicksburg and the subsequent Siege of Jackson from June 15 through July 17, 1863.

Illness

Like many Civil War soldiers, Ashworth faced dangers beyond the battlefield. Disease claimed far more lives during the war than combat itself.

On August 1, 1863, while serving near Muldale, Mississippi, he became seriously ill and was transferred to Covington, Kentucky for treatment.

Circumstances of Death

Despite medical care, Pvt. Ashworth's condition continued to decline.

On January 30, 1864, he died in a military hospital at Lexington, Kentucky.

Legacy

William Ashworth is remembered among Barrington's Civil War fallen and among the thousands of Union soldiers whose service helped preserve the United States during its greatest national crisis.

Though he survived the hardships of campaign and battle, he ultimately became one of the many Civil War soldiers whose lives were claimed by disease contracted during military service.
